# Creating a Great Performance-Focused Music Video

A performance music video emphasizes a musician's or band's live delivery of a song rather than a cinematic storyline. The goal is to capture the energy and authenticity of the artist's performance while delivering a polished, engaging video that supports the music. Below is a step-by-step guide covering pre-production, performance preparation, shooting, audio, editing and release.

## Pre-production planning

### Choose the right song and develop the concept

- **Select your strongest song.** Music video viewers notice the music first. Thomann's music-production guide stresses choosing a track that represents you or your band and grabs attention even without a video. Choose a song that you are confident performing and that resonates with your audience.

- **Define the style and format.** Decide if the video will showcase a straight-ahead performance, a live-concert vibe or a creative twist (e.g., slow reveals or green-screen backgrounds). Plan the video's aspect ratio based on the release platform (landscape for YouTube/TV, portrait for shorts, or square for social ads).

- **Outline the concept and storyboard.** Pre-production saves time and prevents missing shots. MasterClass suggests building a storyboard that maps each shot, camera angle and lens choice. Thomann notes that simply grabbing a camera and shooting often backfires; a storyboard or shot list ensures enough footage and covers all angles. Use the storyboard to communicate your vision to your team.

- **Budget and schedule.** Your concept will dictate crew size, number of locations and gear. MasterClass recommends budgeting for crew members (gaffer, grip, sound technician), actors and equipment. Backstage also notes that professional music videos often cost at least US$2,000 and the average is about US$10,000, but creative low-budget shoots are possible when well planned.

- **Scout locations.** The location sets the tone. Visit potential locations at the time of day you intend to shoot and ensure power access. Thomann advises finding distinctive locations that match your song's mood, whether outdoor, indoor or green-screen. For dynamic looks, Peerspace suggests booking unique venues such as lofts, warehouses or multi-set studios to inspire creativity.

- **Make lists and prepare the crew.** Pinnacle's lighting guide recommends making lists of required gear and personnel and ensuring the crew understands expectations for each scene. A shot list, call sheet and equipment checklist keep production organized.

### Rehearsal and preparation

- **Practice the performance.** A natural on-camera performance comes from rehearsing both the vocals and stage movements. Djaminn stresses that practice helps you feel comfortable in front of the camera and ensures fluid gestures. Rochester Conservatory notes that "winging it" leads to mistakes; rehearse weeks in advance and even perform for friends/family to get honest feedback.

- **Plan blocking and choreography.** If choreography is involved, build rehearsal time into the schedule (MasterClass). Consider how the band members will move relative to the camera and each other.

- **Play the song during shooting.** Backstage recommends playing the song on set so performers know when to enter, move or lip-sync; mismatched lip-sync is distracting. Bring a near-final mix of the track to ensure timing accuracy.

## Performance and stage-presence tips

A performance video lives or dies by the artist's presence. The following practices help your personality shine:

- **Be authentic and have fun.** Djaminn emphasizes that great performances are genuine—don't try to be someone you're not. Audiences can tell if you're enjoying yourself; relaxing, thinking about the lyrics and smiling make the performance more engaging. CareersInMusic advises performers to stop caring what people think and focus on having fun.

- **Engage with the camera/audience.** Make eye contact with the lens, use expressive body language and smile to connect with viewers. Involve the "crowd" by encouraging call-and-response or inviting them to clap along. Stage-presence advice from CareersInMusic suggests involving the crowd (sing-along sections or acknowledging them) and watching great performers to learn techniques.

- **Be energetic and look interesting.** Wear outfits that match your persona and make you stand out. Move around the stage and use the whole performance area to engage different angles. Energy is infectious, and your enthusiasm will show on video.

- **Control facial expressions and posture.** The Cadenza article notes that excessive facial movements (raised eyebrows, darting eyes) and fidgeting can distract viewers. Aim for calm, confident expressions and use clothing—such as fitted jackets or turtlenecks—to help maintain good posture.

- **Collaborate and practice interaction.** Collaborating with other musicians adds variety and chemistry. Djaminn highlights that collaboration exposes you to new styles and improves skills.

## Lighting and set design

Lighting creates the mood of a performance video. Key principles include:

- **Plan lighting in advance.** Light shapes the tone, so decide early whether you'll shoot indoors, outdoors, or at night. Pinnacle advises building storyboards and communicating the lighting design to crew members.

- **Use natural light at golden hour.** Shooting exterior scenes right after sunrise or before sunset (the "golden hour") provides soft, warm light that flatters subjects and gives a cinematic look.

- **Creative lighting techniques:**
  - Indoors, place the fill light higher and slightly off to spill onto the background and create depth.
  - For choreography shots, a top-light behind the subject produces a striking silhouette.
  - Outdoors, avoid harsh midday sun; use reflectors or diffusers to soften sunlight and set correct white balance.
  - NoFilmSchool suggests choosing a color palette and using colored gels or complementary colors to make images pop.
  - For dynamic indoor performance shots, Peerspace recommends draping a fishing net with exposed bulbs over the performance area and gently pulling a rope in rhythm with the music to create moving shadows.

- **Consider moving lights or camera.** Creative light movement (such as the net technique) or camera movement adds energy. Use diffusers, cookies (patterns), and color gels to create texture.

## Equipment and audio considerations

### Cameras and gear

- **Camera choice.** Good performance videos can be shot on smartphones or professional cameras. Thomann notes that smartphones can produce acceptable quality, but professional cameras with interchangeable lenses and large sensors capture cinematic images and allow more control. External monitors and lenses help maintain framing and focus.

- **Multiple takes and angles.** Use more than one camera (or shoot multiple takes) to capture varied angles—close-ups of instruments, medium shots of the vocalist, wide shots of the band. Extra takes give the editor options. Backstage recommends reshooting several times to capture different angles.

- **Drones and dynamic shots.** Drone footage can provide dramatic establishing shots or overhead views and give viewers a sense of scale. Handheld stabilizers, gimbals and dollies enable smooth movement around performers.

### Microphones and audio capture

- **Choose appropriate microphones.** High-quality audio is essential. Audio Network notes that external microphones (brands like Rode, Shure or Sennheiser) deliver cleaner sound than built-in camera mics. Closer mic placement reduces background noise. Options include handheld, lavalier, shotgun and boundary mics; each suits different situations.

- **Use multiple mics and a separate recorder.** Having at least two microphones (e.g., a close mic on the singer and a room mic) provides redundancy and mixing options. Gillespie Productions recommends using a separate recorder for better audio control and quality.

- **Control the recording environment.** Record in a quiet space to minimize hum and echoes. Audio Network suggests treating the room with rugs, curtains and soft furnishings and avoiding hard surfaces. Outdoors, choose wind-sheltered areas and use wind protection on mics.

- **Sync audio and video.** Slate or clap to mark takes and align audio precisely in post-production. Gillespie notes that external recorders or camera audio can act as a guide; careful syncing avoids the distracting mismatch between lips and audio.

- **Monitor and mix audio.** Use headphones to monitor during recording and adjust levels. Later, apply equalization and mixing to give each element (vocals, instruments) its own frequency space. Plugins such as compressors, noise gates and pitch correction can clean up sound.

## Filming the performance

1. **Play the song on set:** Use a speaker or in-ear monitor so performers can follow the track and maintain lip-sync. Backstage emphasizes that mismatched lip-sync is distracting.

2. **Use varied shots:** Start with close-up shots of the singer's face to capture emotion, then slowly pull back to reveal the band, location or crowd. Peerspace calls this the "slow reveal," which creates dramatic surprise. Film performances at different speeds (slow motion or speed-up) to add artistic flair; when using slow motion, adjust playback speed so the musician's lip-sync matches.

3. **Consider one-shot videos:** A single continuous shot can be powerful if the performance is polished. Position the singer to face the camera and move backward or remain static; the effect of prolonged eye contact can be profound.

4. **Include B-roll and cutaways:** Capture extra footage—hands on instruments, crowd reactions, backstage moments or environmental details. Epidemic Sound notes that B-roll adds context and variety.

5. **Capture energy and crowd reactions:** When filming a live performance, record the audience. The DIY Musician guide says talent buyers want to see the performer's quality and the audience's enthusiasm, so pan over the crowd and mix the audio to let viewers hear the music and crowd.

6. **Reshoot and get coverage:** Record multiple takes from different angles. MasterClass advises getting extra takes so editors have options for a cohesive final cut.

7. **Keep the star in focus:** Thomann reminds creators to put the singer or band at the centre; be confident and let the world see you.

## Editing and post-production

- **Organize and map the edit.** Keep notes and organize all ideas in one place (notebook or digital file). Epidemic Sound suggests building a running order or script. Label takes, note strong performances and mark interesting B-roll.

- **Begin with the performance footage.** If the artist appears in the video, edit that footage first and ensure lip-sync accuracy. Then intercut B-roll to provide variety.

- **Match the rhythm of the music.** Edit to the beat; adjust shot length and transitions to follow the song's tempo. Thomann warns that editing too slowly can make great scenes boring, while cutting too quickly can disorient viewers; follow the rhythm of the music.

- **Use transitions and effects thoughtfully.** Apply transitions to connect scenes and maintain continuity. Epidemic Sound encourages experimenting with effects like color grading, speed changes or Ken Burns zooms, but always align them with the song's mood.

- **Color grading and visual effects:** Adjust color and brightness to create a consistent look and match the mood. Use complementary colors or thematic palettes (NoFilmSchool).

- **Software choice:** Epidemic Sound notes that Adobe Premiere Pro is industry standard, but free options like iMovie, OpenShot or DaVinci Resolve can produce professional results.

- **Audio post-production:** Align the final mix of the song with the video. Adjust levels, add EQ, and consider noise reduction or restoration if necessary.

- **Review and refine:** Share draft cuts with the artist and collaborators. MasterClass suggests multiple people will weigh in; refining requires patience and collaboration.

## Release and promotion

- **Finalization and distribution.** Once the video is finished, create a marketing plan. MasterClass recommends teasing snippets before the release, then uploading the full video to video-sharing platforms and social media.

- **SEO and discoverability.** The DIY Musician guide suggests optimizing titles, descriptions and tags so your video appears in search results; include keywords and standardized formatting (e.g., "Artist – Song Title [Official Video]"). Use an eye-catching thumbnail that represents the video and invites clicks.

- **Short-form content and behind-the-scenes.** Create short clips or lyric videos for social media to hook new viewers. Post behind-the-scenes videos or snippets of rehearsal to build anticipation. Short-form videos receive higher engagement than full-length music videos.

## Final thoughts

Creating a compelling performance music video involves more than pointing a camera at a singer. It requires careful planning, rehearsal, creative lighting, thoughtful shot composition, high-quality audio recording, dynamic editing and a promotional strategy. Authenticity and energy are essential—viewers are drawn to performances where artists genuinely enjoy their craft. By following the guidelines above, you can produce a video that not only showcases your musical talent but also connects with audiences and stands out in today's crowded digital landscape.
